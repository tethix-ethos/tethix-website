Air seeds to improve the flow of Collaboration

Another aspect we‚Äôre missing from a lot of the debates on AI is collaboration. AI principles are often developed top-down, without the involvement of diverse stakeholders ‚Äì human and non-human. This is a topic Nate and Mat explored in the presentation Can a culture that supports participatory ethics enhance diversity and inclusion in AI development? delivered at the recent CSIRO Symposium for Diversity and Inclusion in AI.

The stories we tell ‚Äì even ones of cats vs. dogs ‚Äì are so often framed in competitive terms. This is one of a few criticisms we have of the recent, and mostly brilliant, Netflix docuseries Life on Our Planet. It was narrated by Morgan Freeman, which is perhaps enough of a reason to watch. The focus is life through the lens of the five previous mass extinction events, basically defined as events or circumstances that lead to greater than 75% of life on earth being permanently lost.

Freeman frequently repeats that life is defined by competition. But life really doesn‚Äôt seem to be defined only by competition ‚Äì and you can love both cats and dogs, as we do! Oh, and ‚Äì spoiler alert! ‚Äì we‚Äôre in the late stages of the sixth mass extinction event, with greater than 70% of wildlife being lost in just the last 50 years. Freeman suggests that our intelligence will be the key to solving these problems. But this doesn‚Äôt ring true. It‚Äôll be our capacity to act with deep care and genuine wisdom, something that is sorely lacking in our current society.

And we are not just in a relationship with each other, but with the rest of the community of life, a living planet, as Daniel Christian Wahl explored in the excerpt Being a process, and seeing in relationships.
